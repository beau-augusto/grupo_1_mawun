In previous lessons, we've looked at the overall structures used in agile. In this lesson, we'll look at some specific practices used within the team. Agile approaches make some key assumptions about the development team. The team will maintain a positive relationship with the product owner. These two roles must work closely together as we've seen in previous lessons. The team practices in this lesson do not include the product owner. But they do include the coach facilitator, also called the scrum master. Two key terms that describe the team are "cross-functional" and "self-managing."

Cross-functional emphasizes that the team has all the necessary technical skills. They will understand requirements, design, build, and test the software. Self-managing means the team is capable of organizing the work and maintaining a productive environment. This reflects a key principle in agile-- build projects around motivated individuals, give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done.

A key team role in the XP and scrum frameworks is the coach or facilitator. In the scrum framework, this person is called the scrum master. One of their duties is to promote agile practices to all stakeholders within the organization. As a participant in the daily stand up meeting, the coach becomes aware of issues the team is facing and helps to resolve them.

The scrum master and coach embodies the principles of servant leadership. Their role is not to direct the team, but to serve the team by removing obstacles and providing resources. For example, the coach can discuss organizational policy with upper management or work out problems with uncooperative stakeholders.

At the heart of our self-managing teams is the daily stand up. This meeting happens at the beginning of every workday. It is called a stand up because it should be short. If people literally stand up while the meeting is being conducted, they'll be more concise and focused. The goal is to keep the team informed and adjust the daily work plan. Each team member is meant to answer three questions-- what have I accomplished since yesterday? What is my focus today? What obstacles are keeping me from being productive? The scrum master is present for the meeting but only participates if the meeting needs facilitation to keep it moving.

We've seen how a burn up and burn down chart are used to communicate outside of the team. Inside the team, a common practice for planning and tracking is the task board. During planning, the team accepted user stories. These stories can be placed on the task board in the order they'll be performed. Some of these stories are large and require multiple tasks. During the daily stand up meeting, team members move the tasks across the board to show progress. The advantage of task boards is that they are extremely visual. An important principle is that tasks should be small enough to be performed in about one day.

Following that guideline means that the team should see incremental progress every day. The headings in this example are common but not a standard. Teams can add more columns and change the names of the columns to be more relevant to their own work process. The principle of work in process limits is designed to prevent a team member from taking on too much work at one time. The team might set a guideline of only two tasks in process at a time for any one team member.

In our example, team member Able is working on an extra large user story and has three tasks in process at one time. That can prevent focusing and reduce productivity. When the other team members see this, they will encourage Able to focus on one of the tasks and finish it. When team members declare a user story as done, it must really be done, not almost done or done except for a few items. Having a common definition of done, or better yet a checklist, means every team member has the same expectations for completing a user story. It reduces rework and means that you won't have a bunch of unexpected loose ends to clean up at the end of a sprint or release.

Agile techniques originated on software development projects. Continuous integration is a software development technique. The goal is for every user story to be added to a working version of the product. Each user story is tested individually, then added to the current version of the product. The updated product is tested to make sure the new user story didn't introduce errors. At the end of every day, there is a working version of the product. Each user story builds a more complete product. This might seem more difficult to constantly retest the entire product, but it is actually less expensive.

It is better to avoid the build, build, build, then integrate approach. That tends to generate many bugs related to the way the units of code talk to each other. While this technique may not work on non-software projects, it has been proven to be faster and produces fewer software bugs. The final team practice in this lesson is the retrospective. This is a meeting that takes place at the end of every iteration, whether it is a release or a sprint.

The goal is continuous improvement in the way the team works together. The meeting is focused on team and individual interpersonal behavior and team practices, anything that the team can control about the way they work together. By reviewing these simple questions on a routine basis, a team will improve its performance from one sprint to the next. The team practices in this lesson reflect the principles of the Agile Manifesto. They are all built on the core principle, that a team of motivated individuals, need support and trust, and they'll get the job done.

Within all these team practices, we include the coach facilitator, called the scrum master in the scrum framework. The person in this role is a key part of supporting the team. All of these practices work together to enable the team to plan and manage the work, to be productive, and produce high quality working software.
